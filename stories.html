Campfire Tales for Cascade Lake

Read these aloud by the campfire. Use your best spooky voice for the scary ones!

Story Notes

These four stories are sized for cozy campfire reading, with warm whimsy, gentle suspense, and happy endings. Read slowly, pause for sound effects, and let the forest do some of the storytelling for you.

Night 1

Wimbley's First Campout

✨ Whimsical ⏱️ 3 to 4 minutes

On the edge of a piney forest, under a sky the color of toasted marshmallows, lived a small werewolf cub named Wimbley. Wimbley had fluffy ears, a waggy tail he could not quite control, and paws that always seemed one size too big for the rest of him.

One evening, Wimbley's mama said, "Tomorrow we are going camping."

Wimbley's ears popped up. "Camping?" he squeaked. "In the woods? All night? With the twigs and the hooting and the rustling?"

"That is the usual location for camping," said his papa, who was rolling up sleeping bags.

Wimbley gulped. He liked the woods during the day. The woods in daylight had sunbeams, mushrooms, and very polite squirrels. But nighttime woods felt different. Nighttime woods had shadows that looked like giant elbows and noises that sounded like somebody sneezing into a leaf pile.

The next day, Wimbley packed three socks, a toothbrush, a flashlight, a knitted moon hat, and seventeen emergency crackers. "Just in case," he explained.

At the campsite, everything smelled exciting: pine needles, damp earth, warm rocks, and dinner. Wimbley helped set up the tent, although "helped" mostly meant getting tangled in the guy lines and announcing, "I am a very useful forest knot."

When evening came, the forest began to change. The birds quieted. The trees whispered softly. The lake turned shiny and dark like spilled blueberry juice. Wimbley's tummy did a nervous little flip.

"Mama," he whispered, "what if the forest is full of scary things?"

Mama smiled and pointed toward a fern patch. "Let us see who lives here first."

Out from behind the ferns popped a rabbit in a crooked clover crown.

"Good evening," said the rabbit. "I am Juniper. Welcome to camp. Would you like to know where the best moonbeams land?"

Before Wimbley could answer, a round toad waddled by wearing a shiny beetle shell like a backpack. "And if you need directions to the softest moss, I am your fellow," he boomed. "Name's Tumble."

Then a raccoon peeked down from a stump, holding an acorn cup of water. "If anybody needs a bedtime sip, I run the woodland refreshment station after sunset."

Wimbley blinked. None of them seemed scary at all. They seemed... sort of wonderful.

Juniper the rabbit led Wimbley to a patch of silver grass where moonlight spilled between the trees. Tumble the toad showed him how to listen for frog songs near the lake. The raccoon taught him the most important camping rule of all: never sit on a pinecone if you value your tail.

Wimbley laughed so hard he tipped backward into a puff of dry needles.

After dinner, Wimbley sat by the fire with a warm cup of cocoa. He listened to the crackling logs. He watched sparks float up like tiny orange fireflies. Every sound that had seemed spooky before now felt like part of the forest's nighttime song.

Rustle in the bushes? Probably Juniper finding a midnight snack. Plop by the lake? Tumble practicing his belly flop. Soft chitter from above? The raccoon reminding everyone not to forget their socks outside.

"I think," said Wimbley, snuggling into his moon hat, "that the forest is not scary. It is just busy."

Papa smiled. "That is often true of new places."

When Wimbley crawled into the tent, he still heard the hoots and rustles and whispers. But now he imagined all his new forest friends going about their evening business. A rabbit adjusting her clover crown. A toad polishing his beetle-shell backpack. A raccoon checking the lost-and-found for crackers.

Wimbley sat up suddenly. "My emergency crackers!"

From outside came a tiny, polite knock on the tent.

"Refreshment delivery," called the raccoon.

Wimbley giggled, took one cracker for himself, and passed the rest out through the flap. Then he curled up in his sleeping bag, tail warm, heart calm, and eyes growing sleepy.

Just before he drifted off, he whispered, "Best campout ever."

And from somewhere in the dark came the friendly sounds of the forest answering back.

The Glowing Eyes of Cascade Lake

🌙 Spooky ⏱️ 3 to 4 minutes

On the first night at Moran State Park, when the campfire had burned down to glowing coals and the stars were starting to blink awake, a little breeze moved across Cascade Lake.

It made the water shiver.

It made the trees sway.

It made the whole forest feel like it had leaned in to listen.

A boy at camp looked toward the dark lake and froze. "Um," he whispered, "what is that?"

Far across the water, just above the shoreline, two tiny glowing dots appeared.

They hovered in the dark.

They blinked once.

Then twice.

"Eyes," said someone softly.

"Glowing eyes," said someone else, even more softly.

The grown-ups looked out. The dogs lifted their heads. Everyone stared.

The two glowing dots bobbed a little. Then they disappeared.

Silence.

Then, pop, there they were again.

This time they seemed closer.

"Maybe it is the Night Watcher of Cascade Lake," whispered Dad in a deep, spooky voice.

"What does the Night Watcher do?" asked the boy, pulling his blanket up to his chin.

Dad thought for a moment. "Well... perhaps it creeps along the shoreline looking for..." He paused dramatically. "Dropped hot dog buns."

The boy stared. "That is a very silly monster."

"The most dangerous kind," Mom said. "A monster with picnic manners."

Still, the glowing eyes blinked again, and this time even the giggling got a little quieter.

One of the dogs gave a tiny woof. The other dog scooted closer to the camp chairs. The boy squeezed his flashlight, but he did not turn it on yet. He wanted to be brave.

"Maybe we should investigate," Mom said.

So the family stood together and took a few careful steps toward the lake path. Crunch. Crunch. Crunch went the gravel under their shoes.

The glowing eyes vanished.

They stopped.

Then a strange sound drifted across the darkness.

Honk.

Everyone jumped.

"Monsters do not honk," said the boy.

"That is true," Dad admitted. "Unless they are very rude monsters."

They moved a little closer. The moon slid out from behind a cloud and spilled pale light over the water. Suddenly, the mystery was solved.

Floating near the reeds was a very round duck.

Not just any duck. A duck wearing a child-sized glow bracelet around its neck like a sparkly green necklace.

The duck blinked at them. The bracelet glowed. The duck honked again, sounding proud of itself.

The boy burst out laughing. "The glowing eyes were a glowing duck!"

Mom laughed too. "A fashionable glowing duck."

Dad put a hand over his heart. "The terrible Night Watcher of Cascade Lake has been revealed. Guarder of buns. Wearer of lake jewelry."

The duck paddled in a neat little circle as if showing off. Then it dabbled underwater and came up with a soggy leaf on its beak.

Even the dogs relaxed. One wagged. The other sneezed at the duck, which seemed fair.

Back at the campfire, the boy sat down with a grin. The lake did not feel so spooky now. It felt mysterious in a funny kind of way, like the forest might always have one more silly surprise waiting in the dark.

"I am glad we checked," he said.

"Me too," Mom said.

Far out on the water, the duck's bracelet glimmered once more.

Honk.

"Good night, Night Watcher," called the boy.

And from the dark lake came one final proud little honk, as if the duck had accepted the title.

Night 2

Wimbley and the Moonlight Trail

✨ Whimsical ⏱️ 3 to 4 minutes

On Wimbley's second night in the forest, he felt different.

Not bigger exactly. His ears were still fuzzy. His paws were still too large. He still tripped over roots if he forgot to look down.

But he felt less like a visitor and more like someone the woods knew.

After supper, the moon rose round and bright, silvering the pine needles and turning the trail beside camp into a ribbon of pale light.

Wimbley looked at it. The trail looked beautiful.

The trail also looked a tiny bit spooky.

"I want to walk on the moonlight trail," he said, "but only if I do not have to be the bravest one there."

"Excellent plan," said Mama. "Nobody needs to be the bravest one. They only need to be brave enough for the next step."

So Wimbley set out with his lantern, his moon hat, and a peppermint tucked in his pocket for courage. The forest was full of soft nighttime sounds: leaves whispering, crickets chirping, water lapping far off at the lake.

At first Wimbley stayed close to camp. Then he heard a small sound from farther up the trail.

Sniff.

Snuffle.

Snort.

Wimbley stopped. "Hello?" he called.

No answer.

Then a little shape tumbled out from behind a stump and landed in a pile of fern fronds with a squeak.

It was a hedgehog wearing a scarf that was much too long for her.

"Oh dear," she said. "That was not my grand entrance. I am trying to be mysterious."

"You are doing a very nice job of being surprised instead," Wimbley said.

The hedgehog sat up and dusted off her scarf. "My name is Midge. I was following the moonlight trail to Moon Mirror Pond."

"What is Moon Mirror Pond?" asked Wimbley.

Midge gasped. "You have never seen it? It is the place where the moon checks its reflection to make sure it is shining straight."

Wimbley's eyes grew round. "Can anyone go?"

"Certainly," said Midge. "If they are willing to walk quietly and not insult the frogs."

That seemed reasonable, so the two new friends continued along the trail. Wimbley held up his lantern. Midge pointed out glowing mushrooms at the base of logs, owl feathers caught in bark, and tiny tracks in the dust that looked like commas with toes.

Still, every now and then the dark between the trees made Wimbley's heart thump faster.

"What if something jumps out?" he whispered.

Midge considered this. "Then we will likely both yelp. But we will yelp together."

Wimbley smiled. That made the darkness feel smaller.

They rounded one last bend and found a still pond tucked between ferns. Moonlight rested on it so perfectly that it looked like someone had set a silver coin on the water.

"Moon Mirror Pond," Midge announced grandly.

Wimbley stared. It was one of the most beautiful things he had ever seen.

Then, from the bushes behind them, something rustled loudly.

Wimbley jumped. Midge squeaked. Together, exactly as promised, they yelped.

Out of the bushes burst... Juniper the rabbit from the night before, wearing a wreath of grass and carrying three blueberries.

"There you are!" she said. "I brought trail snacks."

Wimbley put a paw to his chest. "You nearly bounced my fur off."

"Sorry," said Juniper cheerfully. "I was trying to be dramatic."

They sat by the pond and shared the blueberries. Wimbley noticed that his heart had stopped racing. He had walked the trail. He had faced the shadows. He had even survived a surprise rabbit.

"I think I know something now," Wimbley said at last.

"What is it?" asked Midge.

Wimbley looked at the moon's reflection and smiled. "Being brave does not mean you are never nervous. It means you keep going kindly, one step at a time, even while your tummy is doing somersaults."

Midge nodded. "That is an extremely useful kind of brave."

When Wimbley returned to camp, his lantern glowed warmly and his tail swung with every step. He slipped into his sleeping bag and whispered into the darkness, "Good night, moonlight trail. I think we are friends now."

Outside, the forest rustled softly, as if agreeing.

The Whispering Trees of Orcas Island

🌙 Spooky ⏱️ 3 to 4 minutes

On the second night at camp, the island air felt cooler.

The campfire popped softly. The stars were bright. Beyond the circle of firelight, the tall trees of Moran State Park stood dark and still.

Then, from somewhere up high in the branches, came a whisper.

Shhhhhh.

The family looked up.

Shhhhhh.

"Did you hear that?" asked the boy.

Before anyone could answer, another whisper slipped through the trees.

Wooooosh.

Tap-tap-tap.

The boy scooted closer to the grown-ups. Even the dogs stared into the dark as if the trees themselves might be trying to say something.

"The Whispering Trees of Orcas Island," Dad murmured in his campfire voice. "Legend says they speak at night."

"What do they say?" the boy asked.

Dad leaned closer. "Nobody knows. Mostly because trees are terrible at speaking clearly."

That earned a small laugh, but then the whispering came again.

Shhhhhh.

Tap-tap-tap-tap.

Something brushed through the branches overhead.

Mom held up a finger. "Listen carefully. Maybe the trees are not whispering. Maybe something in the trees is."

So everybody went quiet. Very quiet.

The campfire crackled. The lake sighed in the distance. Then the sound came once more, from above and a little to the left.

Shhhhhh.

Tap.

Tap-tap.

Tap-tap-tap-tap-tap.

"That sounds less like whispering," Mom said, "and more like... very clumsy drumming."

Dad clicked on a flashlight and aimed it carefully into the branches. For a second, all they saw were needles and bark. Then the beam caught movement.

A tiny shape scrambled along a branch.

Then another.

Then three more.

Squirrels.

Not sleeping squirrels. Not quiet squirrels. Squirrels having what appeared to be a midnight pinecone argument.

One squirrel was dragging a pinecone twice the size of its head. Another was trying to snatch it away. A third was stomping on a hollow branch, making the tap-tap-tap sound like a tiny tree drummer in a band no one had asked for.

The whispering sound came again as the branches swayed and the pine needles brushed together.

Shhhhhh.

The boy blinked. "The whispering trees are just grumpy squirrels?"

"Apparently," said Mom.

Right then, the squirrel with the giant pinecone lost its grip.

The pinecone tumbled down through the branches.

Bink.

Bonk.

Boop.

It landed squarely in Dad's empty camp mug.

Everyone stared for half a second.

Then the whole family burst out laughing.

Up in the tree, the squirrels froze as if caught in the middle of a very embarrassing performance. One gave an indignant chit-chit-chit. Another vanished behind the trunk. The little drummer squirrel thumped the branch one last time, almost as if saying, "Show's over."

The boy laughed so hard he had to wipe his eyes. "They were not whispering scary things. They were just being silly in squirrel language."

"Which, to be fair," Dad said, lifting the pinecone from his mug, "is probably mostly about snacks."

After that, the sounds in the trees did not seem spooky at all. They sounded like island nighttime: wind in the needles, branches brushing, squirrels fussing over pinecones they could barely carry.

The boy leaned back in his chair and listened again.

Shhhhhh.

Tap-tap.

This time he grinned.

"Good night, whispering trees," he called.

From somewhere above came a final chatter and a soft shower of pine needles, as if the squirrels were saying good night too.
